# What is content quality?

Evaluate documentation against six core quality attributes. Think of these not as a checklist but as a rubric: a set of dimensions you can weight and adjust based on your content's maturity and audience.

## The six quality traits

<CardGrid columns={3}>
  <Card title="Accurate">
    Information is factually correct, up-to-date, and technically precise. Code
    examples work as written, and statements can be verified.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Relevant">
    Content directly addresses user needs and goals. Information is appropriate
    for the audience and context, without tangential details.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Complete">
    Content contains all necessary information for users to understand and
    accomplish their goals. All required components are present with sufficient
    context and examples.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Concise">
    Information is expressed efficiently without unnecessary words or
    redundancy. Every sentence serves a purpose and respects the reader's time.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Consistent">
    Terminology, formatting, tone, and style remain uniform throughout. Patterns
    are predictable, making content easier to navigate and understand.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Discoverable">
    Content is easy to find through search, navigation, clear titles, and proper
    organization. Users can locate what they need quickly.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

## Measuring quality is hard

Measuring content quality is hard, especially at scale. The gold standard (hand a doc to someone and see if they can complete the task) doesn't scale. Rather than relying on a single metric, develop a combination of proxy metrics that map to the six quality traits:

- **Feedback & helpfulness** - ratings and especially actionable comments from users
- **Usage data** - traditional web analytics like unique users, page views
- **Freshness & authoring** - last edited dates, last verified timestamps, review cycles

AI adds new signals to track: citations in AI answers, helpfulness ratings from AI chat apps, and feedback on AI-generated responses that use your docs. Be aware of a key challenge: distinguish **retrieval problems** (AI tries to use docs when the answer shouldn't come from docs at all) from **content quality issues** (docs exist but produce inaccurate or unhelpful answers).

## Rethinking CSAT & helpfulness scores

CSAT and helpfulness ratings work well as content freshness signals, but treat them cautiously as primary success metrics:

- **Low sample sizes** produce unreliable data
- **Negativity bias** skews results; dissatisfied users disproportionately respond
- **External factors** like product issues or user expectations can drive poor ratings independent of content quality

Use CSAT as a trigger for content review and updates, not as a standalone quality measure.

## AI-era quality signals

### Defect ratios in AI applications

When docs power AI-driven support or code generation, track these signals:

- **Hallucination rates** traced to documentation gaps
- **Clarification requests** indicating incomplete or ambiguous content
- **Correction frequency** when AI responses require human intervention

### Completeness takes new importance

In AI contexts, document the obvious. AI systems need explicit, comprehensive information and can't infer from context like humans. Edge cases and basic details both feed AI knowledge graphs. Gaps that humans navigate easily become blind spots for AI.
